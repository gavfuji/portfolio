AnteatAir

Overview

Design/Build/Fly is an annual competition hosted by the American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics where participating universities design an RC aircraft to meet specific design criteria described by a ruleset released in August and attend a fly-off held in either Wichita, Kansas or Tucson, Arizona in April. The 2024-2025 competition presented two unique design challenges: the inclusion of detachable wing-mounted payloads and the deployment of a glider into a designated target area. I'd wager the Design/Build/Fly team at UC Irvine faced a third challenge: a concerning lack of returning members. Many of the experienced members from the previous year graduated, leaving a team of mostly new members. Without anybody else to lead the way, I took on the role of project manager without knowing what it entailed. The road ahead was uncertain, but I was determined to navigate it for the sake of the team. Throughout weeks and weeks of late nights, long meetings, and a fair share of setbacks, I helped build a plane that made it all the way to the skies of Tucson, Arizona. More importantly, I helped build a team that I am proud to be a part of.

Design

Unlike some of my personal projects, the design of AnteatAir was strictly governed by the competition ruleset. These rules determined the maximum wingspan, allowable configurations, electronics setup, and other design parameters. Most notably, the performance of the aircraft was evaluated based on scoring equations tied to each mission. These equations factored in elements such as payload weight, laps flown, and glider accuracy to provide an overall score used to determine the top-placing teams. One mission was heavily weighted towards the maximum weight the aircraft could carry using wing-mounted payloads, while another prioritized the weight of the glider and its accuracy in hitting a target. A combination of hand calculations, optimization scripts, simulations, and real flight tests were used to arrive at a final design.

The final design was a 6-ft wingspan, 10-inch chord, mid-wing conventional aircraft with a tricycle configuration landing gear. The aircraft was powered by a 4500mAh 6s LiPo battery, a SunnySky X4130-8 380kV motor, and an 18x10E propeller. The wings, tails, and fuselage were constructed using built-up balsa with plywood and carbon fiber reinforcements. The wings were detachable to facilitate transportation and access panels were incorporated into the fuselage for battery and electronics access. The wing-mounted payloads were plastic soda bottles attached using a custom 3D-printed mounting system for rapid installation and removal. The glider was a modified off-the-shelf foam glider with fixed control surfaces to initiate an controlled descent pattern and an onboard LED-system for visbility. The glider was released using an internal servo-actuated mechanism connected to hooks on the glider's underside.

Aerodynamics

As the aerodynamics lead, I was responsible for the aerodynamic design and analysis of the aircraft. I performed scoring analysis, preliminary sizing, and performance validation using a combination of hand calculations, MATLAB scripts, and XFLR5 simulations. I developed an optimization program in MATLAB that combined the scoring equations with early performance estimates and aerodynamic equations to determine the highest-scoring balance of design parameters. A separate sensitivity analysis graphically compared the relative impact of variations in each of the design parameters to validate the optimization results. The initial aircraft was sized based off of these results and techniques described in rudimentary aircraft design textbooks. XFLR5 was used to simulate the aircraft's aerodynamic performance and stability characteristics, allowing for changes to the airfoil selection, wing geometry, and tail sizing prior to building a physical prototype. Flight testing allowed for real-world performance validation and final adjustments to further increase performance.

Project Management

Although I picked up a lot of technical skills throughout the year, I think my greatest takeaway is how much effort it takes to be a good leader. As project manager, I handled meetings, budgeting, scheduling, task delegation, communication with advisors, and any unexpected issues that arose along the way. Early on, I struggled to find my footing in this new role. At the first meeting, I faced a room full of new members who were unsure of what to expect, and I was unsure of what to deliver. My mind was focused on how much time it would take to build a plane and not on how to build a lasting team. I spent too much time doing things by myself, and I felt the consequences. I burned out and spiraled into feelings of inadequacy, self-doubt, and frustration. It wasn't until I took a step back and take a moment to breathe that I was able to turn things around. The team came first, and results would follow. During crunch time, I stepped away completely from any hands-on work and focused entirely on keeping the team organized, motivated, and on track. Before I knew it, we were on our way to Tucson, on a journey I'd be hard-pressed to forget.

Results

I'd love to talk about producing great results, but that was far from reality. We placed 59th of 112 competing teams, completing only two of the four missions. The road was riddled with too many mistakes, many of which were my own. Yet, I am hopeful for the future of the team and for good reason; this year we have seventeen returning members, each with far more experience in the project than my past self. We're already making smarter decisions and working harder to make this year better than the last.
