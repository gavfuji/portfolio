Pneumatic Rover

Overview

As part of a class project for a course at UC Irvine, I worked in a team of four to construct an autonomous rover propelled using a piston and tire pressure reservoir. The rover was designed to navigate a pre-defined course using an onboard magnetometer for heading reference. As the programming lead, I was responsible for developing the control algorithms and software integration.

Design

The frame of the rover was constructed using various types of wood, mounting brackets, and screws. Electronics were mounted directly onto the frame and included an Arduino Uno, magnetometer, solenoid, servo, limit switch, and battery. The pneumatic system consisted of a tire and tubing routed through the solenoid to a piston. The servo controlled a parallel-bar steering linkage and the limit switch was mounted near the rear wheel to act as a rotary encoder.

Two control systems were developed depending on the level of required precision. The first was an partial open-loop system that tracked distance traveled using the limit switch and used fixed headings to navigate the course. While this system was simple to implement and offered limited success, slight course variations led to significant deviations from the intended path. The second system was a closed-loop feedback controller that used the magnetometer to correct heading errors in real-time. This system tracked the rover's position using dead reckoning and adjusted the steering angle using a PID controller to minimize heading error. To further improve performance, a simple digital filter was applied to the magnetometer readings to reduce noise and PID gains were tuned through testing.

Results

Unfortunately, the rover was unable to compete in the final competition due to a size violation. However, the rover demonstrated full functionality during testing and passed all other validation checks. Collected data from testing demonstrated the accuracy of distance measurements and the effectiveness of the heading correction algorithm.
